Geoengineering

Solar Radiation Modification

Geoengineering is a way we could try to cool the planet or remove carbon dioxide from the air. One approach is Solar Radiation Modification (SRM), which includes reflecting sunlight back to space to cool Earth. Techniques include spraying reflective particles into the atmosphere or enhancing cloud brightness. While SRM could potentially lower global temperatures quickly, it does not addres the root cause of climate change (excessive greenhouse gas emissions) and instead acts like a temporary fix. Currently, SRM methods are largely theoretical and have not been implemented on a global scale.

Carbon dioxide Removal

Another approach is Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR), which aims to actually reduce carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. It is performed by planting lots of trees, using machines that capture carbon dioxide, or fertilizing ocens to help tiny plants absorb carbon. While slower to show effects than SRM, CDR targets the root cause of climate change. Some small projects exist, but large-scale implementatio is still theoretical

When could it work?

SRM could have rapid effects in months to a few years, while CDR takes decades to noticeably reduce carbon dioxide levels. However, all methods carry uncertainties, and we do not fully know the unintended consequences. Since most of the methods are theoretical, these timelines are estimates and not guarantees.

Should we?

Geoengineering might help slow climate change, but it raises serious ethical questions. Who should decide if and when these methods are used? a single person? a country? the UN? Relying on geoengineering could reduce the urgency to cut emmissions, but what if this is a shortcut that will backfire? What if one region benefits while another suffers unintended consequences like droughts or floods?

Ask yourself: if we could implement a fix, would you take the risk, knowing the outcomes might hit someone else severely? While this solution seems helpful, we currently lack the data to say with certainty that it doesn't cause more problems than it solves. These questions highlight why geoengineering is complex and controversial.

The decisions around these questions just as important as the science itself.
